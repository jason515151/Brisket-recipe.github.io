Introduction

What You're Making

Texas-style smoked beef brisket — a bark-coated, smoke-ringed, melt-in-your-mouth masterpiece. This is the king of barbecue.

🥩🔥

📋 What You Need

  • Brisket — 12-16 lb whole packer (point + flat)
  • Smoker — offset, pellet, kamado, or kettle
  • Wood — oak, hickory, pecan, or mesquite
  • Rub — coarse salt + coarse black pepper (50/50)
  • Spritz — apple juice or apple cider vinegar
  • Thermometer — leave-in probe + instant-read
  • Butcher paper — unwaxed, for the wrap
  • Patience — the most important ingredient

🥩 Choosing Your Brisket

  • Grade: USDA Prime or Wagyu for best results. Choice works too.
  • Size: 12-16 lbs is ideal. Bigger = more forgiving.
  • Trim: Leave 1/4" fat cap. Remove hard fat. Square it up.
  • Feel: Should be flexible, not stiff. Good marbling throughout.
  • Fresh: Not frozen if possible. If frozen, thaw 3-5 days in fridge.
Step 1

Preparation

Do this the night before. Good prep makes perfect brisket.

🌙 12+ hours before cook

Trim the brisket

  • Flip fat-side down. Trim fat cap to 1/4" uniform thickness.
  • Remove the hard fat seam between point and flat.
  • Square off ragged edges — they'll burn.
  • Flip over. Remove silver skin from the meat side.
  • Shape it aerodynamic — rounded edges, no sharp corners.
🌙 12+ hours before cook

Season (the 50/50 rub)

  • 16 mesh black pepper — 1 part
  • Coarse kosher salt — 1 part
  • Optional: garlic powder, paprika, cayenne (go easy)
  • Apply generously — it should look like sandy beach, not bare skin.
  • Pat it in. Don't rub it off. Let it adhere.
🌙 12+ hours before cook

Rest in fridge (uncovered)

Place brisket on a wire rack over a sheet tray. Leave uncovered in the fridge overnight. This dries the surface — dry surface = better bark.

🔥 1 hour before smoke

Bring to temp

Pull brisket from fridge 1 hour before it goes on the smoker. Let it start warming up. Meanwhile, get your smoker running.

Step 2

The Cook

Low and slow. Don't rush it. Don't peek too much.

🔥 0:00 — Fire up

Get the smoker to 225°F

  • Use oak, hickory, or pecan for the fuel/wood chunks.
  • Stabilize temp at 225°F before the brisket goes on.
  • Thin blue smoke — not thick white smoke.
🔥 0:30 — Brisket on

Place fat-side down (or up — debate rages)

  • Fat-side toward the heat source.
  • Insert leave-in probe into the thickest part of the flat.
  • Close the lid. Don't open it for at least 3 hours.
🔥 3:00 — First spritz

Spritz with apple juice every 45-60 min

Light mist. Don't soak it. This adds flavor and keeps the surface from drying out before the bark sets.

🔥 ~8:00 — The Stall (165°F-170°F)

The stall is normal. Do not panic.

Internal temp will plateau as moisture evaporates. This can last 1-4 hours. Do not crank up the heat. Let it ride. This is where collagen breaks down into gelatin.

🔥 ~165°F-170°F — The Wrap

Wrap in butcher paper

  • Lay out a 3-foot sheet of unwaxed butcher paper.
  • Place brisket in the center. Fold one side over, then the other.
  • Roll it tight like a burrito. Tuck the ends.
  • Probe back through the paper into the flat.
  • Why wrap: It pushes through the stall faster, retains moisture, and the paper breathes so bark stays firm.
🔥 ~195°F — Probe test

Start checking for tenderness

Insert a probe into the flat. It should slide in like room-temp butter — zero resistance. If it's still a little tight, wait. Every brisket is different.

🔥 200°F-205°F — Done

Target internal temp: 203°F in the flat

The point will be a bit hotter — that's fine. When the probe slides in like butter, it's done. Do not cook to just a number — cook to tenderness. But 203°F is your target range.

Step 3

Rest & Serve

This step is not optional. Resting is as important as the cook.

🧊 Rest (2-4 hours minimum)

  • Wrap the brisket in a fresh sheet of butcher paper.
  • Wrap again in plastic wrap or place in a disposable foil pan.
  • Wrap the whole thing in a towel.
  • Place in a cooler (no ice) — this is a faux cambro.
  • It will stay hot for 4+ hours.
  • This relaxes the meat and redistributes juices.

🔪 Slicing

  • Find the grain direction on the flat (it changes at the point).
  • Separate the point from the flat at the fat seam.
  • Slice the flat against the grain in pencil-thick slices.
  • Slice the point against its grain — or cube it for burnt ends.
  • Pro move: slice only what you'll eat immediately.
"The difference between good brisket and great brisket is the rest. Don't skip it." — every pitmaster ever
Pro Tips

Pitmaster Secrets

🔥 Fire Management

  • Stabilize at 225°F before the meat goes on.
  • Small, frequent adjustments beat big swings.
  • Keep your wood chunks dry. Store them inside.
  • Clean ash every 4-6 hours for clean airflow.
  • Use a water pan in the smoker for thermal mass and humidity.

🥩 Bark Building

  • Dry surface = good bark. Don't spritz too early (wait 3+ hours).
  • The 50/50 rub is classic for a reason. Don't overcomplicate it.
  • Mustard or hot sauce as a binder? Optional. The salt draws out moisture and the rub sticks anyway.
  • If the bark is perfect but not done cooking yet, wrap in paper — this stops the bark from getting any darker.

🚫 Common Mistakes

  • Cooking to temp, not tenderness. 203°F is a target, not a rule.
  • Slicing too early. The meat needs to rest. 2 hours minimum. 4 is better.
  • Slicing with the grain. It'll be shoe leather. Always against the grain.
  • Opening the smoker too much. Every peek costs 10-15 minutes of cook time.
  • Not trimming enough. Hard fat doesn't render. Cut it out.
  • Using too much smoke. Thin blue smoke. White smoke = creosote = bitter brisket.

🍖 Leftovers (if you have any)

  • Burnt ends — cube the point, sauce it, back on the smoker for 1 hour.
  • Brisket tacos — chop leftovers, warm with salsa verde.
  • Brisket hash — dice with potatoes, onions, peppers. Fry in a cast iron.
  • Freeze — vacuum seal sliced brisket with a splash of beef broth.
